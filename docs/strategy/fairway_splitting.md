# Current Method for Fairway Splitting and Integration

This document outlines the methodology used in the `fis-crawler` for schematizing lock and bridge structures into the fairway network graph. It serves as a baseline to understand how fairways are split, how topological nodes are introduced, and where the current method struggles with complex scenarios.

## Current Method for Fairway Splitting

The fundamental operation for inserting a structure (lock or bridge) into a fairway is defined in `fis.utils.process_fairway_geometry`. This method calculates how much of the fairway needs to be "reserved" or "cut out" to make room for the structure's detailed internal routing graph.

**Method and Parameters:**
1. **Projection:** The structure's centroid is projected onto the fairway linestring using an accurate metric projection (`EPSG:28992`) to find its distance along the fairway (`projected_dist`).
2. **Buffer Calculation (`max_offset`):**
   - For locks, the base offset is `(max_chamber_length / 2) + 50` meters. This defines the minimum symmetric distance upstream and downstream required to fit the lock.
   - If bridge openings (`openings_data`) are embedded within or near the structure, the method dynamically expands the offset to encompass the farthest opening projection, adding a margin of up to 100 meters (or whatever space is left on the linestring).
3. **Line Splicing:** The fairway linestring is cut at `projected_dist - max_offset` and `projected_dist + max_offset`. This results in two new geometry objects:
   - `geometry_before_wkt`: The upstream approach fairway segment.
   - `geometry_after_wkt`: The downstream continuation fairway segment.
4. **Node Generation:** The end coordinate of `geometry_before_wkt` becomes the `split_point` and the start coordinate of `geometry_after_wkt` becomes the `merge_point`.

## Inserting Locks

Locks are processed in `fis.lock.core` and `fis.lock.graph`.

### Inserting a lock with a single chamber
- The `split_point` and `merge_point` generated by the fairway splitting logic become the topological `lock_{id}_split` and `lock_{id}_merge` nodes.
- `find_chamber_doors` calculates the nearest points on the chamber geometry to the split and merge points, designating them as `chamber_start` and `chamber_end`.
- Three sequential edges are generated to route traffic through the lock:
  1. **Approach:** `lock_split` -> `chamber_start`
  2. **Route:** `chamber_start` -> `chamber_end`
  3. **Exit:** `chamber_end` -> `lock_merge`

### Inserting a lock with two chambers
- The `max_offset` is determined by the longest chamber in the lock complex.
- Both chambers share the **same** `lock_{id}_split` and `lock_{id}_merge` nodes.
- Each chamber gets its own distinct `chamber_start` and `chamber_end` nodes.
- The graph naturally branches at the `split` node, routes through parallel chamber edges, and converges at the `merge` node.

## Inserting Bridges

Bridges are processed using a similar paradigm in `fis.bridge.core` and `fis.bridge.graph`.

### Inserting a bridge with one opening
- The fairway is split based on the bridge's geometry footprint, generating a `bridge_{id}_split` and `bridge_{id}_merge` node.
- The opening geometry's extreme points relative to the fairway direction become `opening_{id}_start` and `opening_{id}_end`.
- Three edges are generated:
  1. `bridge_split` -> `opening_start`
  2. `opening_passage`: `opening_start` -> `opening_end`
  3. `opening_end` -> `bridge_merge`

### Inserting a bridge with two openings
- The structure shares a single `bridge_{id}_split` and `bridge_{id}_merge` node.
- The graph branches at the `split` node into multiple parallel `opening_passage` edges (one for each physical opening) and converges back at the `merge` node.

## Inserting Locks with Bridges (Embedded Bridges)

Bridges that fall within the geographic footprint of a lock complex (e.g., Krammersluizen, Volkeraksluizen) are handled by `fis.dropins.embedded`. Instead of splitting the fairway twice independently, the bridge is injected into the lock's internal routing graph.

### Inserting a bridge over a lock chamber
- After generating the basic lock topology, `_index_chamber_routes_and_openings` identifies bridge openings whose centroids fall within 50 meters of any lock edge (`approach`, `route`, or `exit`).
- If a bridge aligns with the `chamber_route` edge, the original chamber edge is removed.
- `_splice_chamber_route_for_bridge` cuts the edge at the opening's projected distance.
- The bridge's original standalone `split` and `merge` nodes are deleted.
- The opening's `start` and `end` nodes are spliced directly into the middle of the chamber edge.
  - **Topology:** `chamber_start` -> `opening_start` -> `opening_end` -> `chamber_end`

### Inserting a bridge before or after a lock chamber
- If the bridge is located physically before the lock chamber (on the approach) or after the lock chamber (on the exit lane), the logic is functionally identical to inserting it over the chamber.
- The logic identifies the closest edge (`chamber_approach` or `chamber_exit`) and splices it.
- **Topology (After):** `chamber_end` -> `opening_start` -> `opening_end` -> `lock_merge`
- **Topology (Before):** `lock_split` -> `opening_start` -> `opening_end` -> `chamber_start`

## Complex Situations

The current method assumes a simple, sequential topological environment (upstream fairway -> single complex -> downstream fairway). This breaks down in complex real-world scenarios due to the reliance on symmetric, centroid-based buffering and assumed linear flow.

### Inserting a lock complex with multiple locks
- **Example:** Oranjesluizen (Complex 50750 / 59464015). A single administrative complex contains multiple physically distinct locks (Prins Willem-Alexandersluis and the older Oranjesluizen). The actual fairway network branches into two distinct paths *before* reaching the lock approaches.
- **Current Behavior:** The code calculates a single, massive `max_offset` for the entire complex from a shared centroid, creating a single `lock_split` node and a single `lock_merge` node for all locks in the complex.
- **The Flaw:** This approach completely ignores pre-existing topological fairway branches (e.g., existing junction nodes). It creates incorrect, straight-line "approach" edges that bypass the actual physical fairway branches, misrepresenting the topological reality of separated waterways.

### Inserting a chamber with a junction node in the lock chamber
- **Example:** Sluis Weurt (Lock 49032). This lock features an embedded bridge opening (5835) and an existing fairway junction node (`8864190`) located *inside* the chamber geometry itself.
- **Current Behavior:** 
  1. The dynamic buffer expansion in `process_fairway_geometry` pushes the `lock_split` node far upstream to accommodate the internal bridge and the large chamber size symmetrically.
  2. The integration logic places the `chamber_start` door based purely on the distance from this extreme `split_point`.
  3. The pre-existing junction node (`8864190`) remains anchored to its original physical coordinate and topological rank.
- **The Flaw:** Because the `max_offset` pushes the split point back symmetricallyâ€”regardless of actual physical requirementsâ€”the dynamically generated `chamber_start` node is often placed topologically *behind* the original split or junction node relative to the flow of traffic. This results in tangled topology where the sequence of nodes (`lock_split` -> `chamber_start` -> `internal_junction` -> `bridge`) becomes self-intersecting or falls out of order.
